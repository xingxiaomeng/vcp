# Constraints

## Limitations of the Paper

- **L01 — Variance decomposition is descriptive, not causal**: Partitioning variance into student vs. school components does not by itself establish causal mechanisms. Student characteristics may partially reflect prior school effects.
- **L01a — Gender/inequality argument is untested**: The deductive argument that men and women should have similar IQs but different achievement in gender-unequal educational systems (p.7) is a conceptual proposal. No direct empirical test is presented.
- **L01b — Genetic prediction is aspirational**: The claim that "it will be possible, one day soon, to use genetic information to accurately predict individual IQs" (p.9) represents a future projection, not an established finding.
- **L02 — Estimate range for teacher effects is wide (1%–8%)**: The paper reports teacher-attributable variance ranging from ~1% (Coleman report, direct estimate) to 8% (Byrne et al., twin study upper bound). This range spans nearly an order of magnitude.
- **L03 — Intelligence measurement is imperfect**: Standardized intelligence tests capture only a subset of cognitive abilities. The latent g factor is itself a statistical abstraction.
- **L04 — Heyneman-Loxley effect in poorer countries limits generalization**: The claim that school effects are small applies primarily to developed economies. The variance structure in low-income countries differs.
- **L05 — Thought experiment oversimplifies actual educational contexts**: The paper's central thought experiment assumes random assignment, identical content, fixed instructional time, and no interaction effects — conditions not met in actual education.
- **L06 — Causal direction evidence is limited**: Cross-lagged panel studies provide only temporal evidence of causation. Alternative causal structures (e.g., third variables) cannot be ruled out.
- **L07 — Singular focus on variance partitioning**: Other important metrics (effect sizes, growth trajectories, distributional changes) may tell different stories about teacher impact.
- **L08 — Genetic/environmental covariance**: Student characteristics and school environments are not independent — genetic predispositions influence both the environments students select and how schools treat them.

## Assumptions Made in the Paper

- **A01** (from problem.md): Variance proportion sets upper bound for intervention potential.
- **A02**: Value-added measures provide reasonable estimates of teacher effects.
- **A03**: General intelligence (g) is a valid construct for understanding academic achievement.
- **A04**: Variance can be meaningfully partitioned into student and school components, though these interact.
- **A05**: The developed-economy pattern generalizes across similar contexts.
- **A06**: Cross-lagged designs provide meaningful causal evidence.

## Boundaries of Claims

- Claims apply primarily to developed economies (per capita income > ~$16,000)
- Claims are about total variance proportions, not effect sizes of specific interventions
- Claims do not assert that teachers have zero effect — only that their potential effect is bounded
- The paper does not argue against the importance of good teaching; it argues against the misallocation of research focus
