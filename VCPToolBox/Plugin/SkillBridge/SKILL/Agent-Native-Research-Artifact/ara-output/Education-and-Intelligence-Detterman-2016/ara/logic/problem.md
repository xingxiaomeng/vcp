# Problem

## Observations

**O01**: In the United States and much of the world, teachers are blamed or praised for the academic achievement of their students, implying teachers are entirely responsible for educational outcomes.
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.1, Abstract / opening paragraph
- Support: "Reading some educational research it is easy to get the idea that teachers are entirely responsible for the success of educational outcomes."

**O02**: The variance in educational outcomes attributable to schools and teachers has been known to be small for at least 50 years, yet this knowledge has been largely ignored by educators.
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.1
- Support: "I argue that this idea is badly mistaken. Teachers are responsible for a relatively small portion of the total variance in students' educational outcomes. This has been known for at least 50 years."

**O03**: Educational practice has not fundamentally changed since the beginning of recorded history. The greatest educational innovations have been the printed book and the blackboard.
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.2
- Support: "Education has not changed from the beginning of recorded history… Historically, the greatest educational innovations have been the printed book and the blackboard."

**O04**: There is a substantial body of unambiguous research showing the dominant role of student characteristics in academic achievement, but this evidence is routinely marginalized in educational policy and discourse.
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.3
- Support: "There is a very large body of research that is unambiguous about the role of the student at every level of education."

**O05**: The focus of educational reform and research has been disproportionately on teachers and schools (factors thought to be easily changeable), rather than on student characteristics (perceived as less amenable to modification).
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.2
- Support: "The main reason people seem to ignore the research is that they concentrate on the things they think they can change easily and ignore the things they think are unchangeable."

**O06**: Intelligence (general cognitive ability) accounts for between half and two-thirds of the total variance in academic achievement — 6 to 9 times more than teachers.
- Source: Detterman (2016), p.7
- Support: "Intelligence accounts for somewhere between half and two-thirds of the total variance in academic achievement… Intelligence accounts for 6 to 9 times more variance than teachers."

## Gaps

**G01**: Why has education research and policy for 50+ years systematically ignored the overwhelming evidence that student characteristics — particularly intelligence — account for the vast majority of variance in academic achievement?

**G02**: If teachers account for only 1%–8% of total variance and schools for ~10%, what is the upper bound of achievable improvement through school/teacher-focused reform?

**G03**: What would a research agenda centered on understanding student characteristics (intelligence, genetics, brain, cognition) look like, and could it produce meaningful educational improvement?

**G04**: The Heyneman-Loxley effect suggests schools matter more in poorer countries. Does this pattern hold, and is it diminishing as schooling availability increases in those countries?

## Key Insight

The paper's central insight is that the allocation of research and policy attention is almost exactly inverse to the actual distribution of variance in academic achievement. If we want to improve education, we must understand what drives the 90% of variance that belongs to students — and the most powerful known driver within that 90% is general intelligence.

## Assumptions

- **A01**: The proportion of variance attributable to a factor sets an upper bound on the potential effect of interventions targeting that factor.
- **A02**: Value-added (VA) measures of teacher quality, which control for prior student achievement, provide a reasonable (though imperfect) estimate of teacher effects.
- **A03**: General intelligence (g) as measured by standardized tests is a valid and meaningful construct for understanding academic achievement.
- **A04**: The total variance in academic achievement can be meaningfully partitioned into student-associated and school/teacher-associated components, though these categories are not entirely independent.
- **A05**: Developed economies (per capita income > ~$16,000) show a relatively stable pattern of variance distribution that generalizes across similar contexts.
- **A06**: Cross-lagged correlation designs can provide meaningful causal evidence about the direction of effect between intelligence and achievement.
