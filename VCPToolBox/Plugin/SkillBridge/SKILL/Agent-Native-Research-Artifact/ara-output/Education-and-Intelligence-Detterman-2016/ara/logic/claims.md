# Claims

## C01: Teachers account for a small fraction of total variance in academic achievement
- **Statement**: Teachers account for no more than 1%–8% of the total variance in student academic achievement across all levels of education (primary, secondary, and higher education).
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A randomized controlled trial in which teachers of known quality are randomly assigned to classes of students yields a teacher-attributable variance component consistently exceeding 10% of total achievement variance, OR a comprehensive meta-analysis of comparable studies finds teacher variance systematically above 10%.
- **Proof**: [E01, E02, E03, E04, E05, E06]
- **Dependencies**: [A01, A02, A04]
- **Tags**: teacher effects, variance decomposition, school effects
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21
- **Additional support**: Teacher-induced learning gains show low persistence — 75% or more fade within one year (Jacob, Lefgren, & Sims, 2010). Teacher training averages $18,000/year with limited effectiveness (The New Teacher Project, 2015). Teachers reach maximum effectiveness after ~5 years and then plateau. Only 7 of 77 IES-funded RCTs (9%) showed positive effects (Coalition for Evidence-Based Policy, 2013).

## C02: Student characteristics account for approximately 90% of variance in academic achievement
- **Statement**: In developed economies, student-associated characteristics account for approximately 90% (range 80%–98%) of the total variance in academic achievement.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A large-scale (n > 10,000) study in a developed economy using multilevel modeling finds that student-level variables (demographics, prior achievement, cognitive ability) account for less than 70% of the total variance in academic achievement after controlling for school-level variables.
- **Proof**: [E01, E02, E03, E04, E05]
- **Dependencies**: [C01, A01, A04]
- **Tags**: student characteristics, variance decomposition, developed economies
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21

## C03: General intelligence accounts for the majority of the student-attributable variance
- **Statement**: General cognitive ability (intelligence) accounts for between 50% and 80% of the student-attributable variance in academic achievement, or approximately 34%–54% of total variance — 6 to 9 times more than teachers.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A study using latent variable modeling with both intelligence tests and academic achievement measures in a large representative sample (n > 5,000) finds that the squared correlation between g and achievement g is less than 0.25 (i.e., intelligence accounts for < 25% of achievement variance), OR that other student characteristics (e.g., grit, motivation, SES) collectively account for more variance than intelligence.
- **Proof**: [E07, E08, E09, E10]
- **Dependencies**: [C02, A03]
- **Tags**: general intelligence, cognitive ability, academic achievement, variance
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21

## C04: Intelligence is a causal driver of academic achievement, not vice versa
- **Statement**: Cross-lagged panel studies show that intelligence measured at Time 1 predicts academic achievement at Time 2, but prior academic achievement does not significantly predict later intelligence, indicating a directional causal relationship from intelligence to achievement.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A well-powered (n > 2,000) cross-lagged panel study with at least two waves of testing finds that the cross-lagged path from achievement (T1) → intelligence (T2) is statistically significant and at least as large as the path from intelligence (T1) → achievement (T2).
- **Proof**: [E11, E12, E13]
- **Dependencies**: [A06]
- **Tags**: causality, cross-lagged, intelligence, achievement, direction of effect
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21

## C05: The Heyneman-Loxley effect is diminishing as school availability increases in poorer countries
- **Statement**: The finding that schools account for a larger proportion of variance in poorer countries (the Heyneman-Loxley effect) is diminishing over time as access to schooling expands in those countries, converging toward the developed-country pattern.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A cross-national study using recent (post-2010) data from low-income countries finds that school-level variance consistently exceeds 40% of total achievement variance and shows no trend of decrease over time.
- **Proof**: [E02, E03]
- **Dependencies**: [C02]
- **Tags**: Heyneman-Loxley effect, developing countries, school effects
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21

## C06: Educational reform should prioritize understanding student characteristics, particularly intelligence
- **Statement**: Significant educational improvement requires understanding the infrastructure of intelligence (genes → brain → behavior), and this understanding is achievable in the foreseeable future.
- **Status**: hypothesis
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: Fifty years of focused research on the genetics, neuroscience, and cognition of intelligence yields no actionable insights for educational practice, OR alternative research programs (e.g., teacher effectiveness, instructional design) demonstrate consistently larger effect sizes than interventions informed by intelligence research.
- **Proof**: [E14, E15, E16]
- **Dependencies**: [C01, C02, C03]
- **Tags**: educational reform, intelligence research, policy implications
- **Last revised**: 2016-10-21
