# Concepts

## Value-Added (VA) Measures
- **Definition**: A method of evaluating teacher quality by estimating the contribution of a teacher to student learning, controlling for students' prior achievement and other background factors. A teacher's VA is the difference between observed student achievement and the achievement predicted based on prior performance.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.1
- **Role in paper**: VA measures provide the basis for estimating teacher effects on student achievement, and set the upper bound for how much variance teachers can explain.

## Heyneman-Loxley Effect
- **Definition**: The finding that school quality accounts for a larger proportion of variance in academic achievement in low-income countries compared to high-income countries. Named after Heyneman and Loxley (1983).
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.3, citing Heyneman & Loxley (1983)
- **Role in paper**: Used to qualify the general claim that school effects are small — this holds primarily in developed economies, though the effect is diminishing in poorer countries as schooling availability increases.

## General Intelligence (g)
- **Definition**: A latent construct representing the common variance shared across diverse cognitive ability tests. Also referred to as general cognitive ability. In this paper, operationalized through tests like the Cognitive Abilities Test (CAT), Raven's Progressive Matrices, WISC, and hierarchical factor models.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016); Spearman (1904)
- **Role in paper**: The central explanatory construct — the single most important known variable accounting for variance in academic achievement.

## Cross-Lagged Panel Correlation
- **Definition**: A statistical method using the logic of temporal precedence to infer causal direction between two variables. Both variables are measured at two time points; if variable X at T1 predicts variable Y at T2 more strongly than Y at T1 predicts X at T2, a causal path from X to Y is inferred.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.7
- **Role in paper**: Used to argue that intelligence causes academic achievement rather than the reverse.

## P-FIT (Parieto-Frontal Integration Theory)
- **Definition**: A neurobiological theory of intelligence proposing that intelligence depends on the integration of information across parietal and frontal brain regions. Extended by Basten, Hilger, and Fiebach (2015).
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.9, citing Jung & Haier (2007)
- **Role in paper**: Provides the neuroscience foundation for understanding the biological infrastructure of intelligence.

## VPR Model (Verbal-Perceptual-Image Rotation)
- **Definition**: A structural model of human intelligence proposed by Johnson and Bouchard (2005a, 2005b) as an alternative to the Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) model. Claims that cognitive abilities are organized around Verbal, Perceptual, and Image Rotation dimensions rather than Fluid and Crystallized intelligence.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.9, citing Johnson & Bouchard (2005a, 2005b)
- **Role in paper**: Indicates progress in understanding the structure of intelligence through statistically validated models.

## Gene × Environment Interaction
- **Definition**: The phenomenon where the effect of genetic endowment on phenotypic outcomes depends on environmental context. Optimal development requires matching individuals to environments well-suited to their genetic predispositions.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.9, citing Asbury & Plomin (2013)
- **Role in paper**: Provides a practical pathway for applying intelligence research to educational practice — matching students to appropriate learning environments.

## Flynn Effect
- **Definition**: The observed rise in raw IQ scores over generations (approximately 3 points per decade). Named after James Flynn (1984).
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.7, citing Flynn (1984); Tuddenham (1948)
- **Role in paper**: Used to discuss the possibility that environmental factors can influence measured intelligence, while noting that fluid intelligence may not be changing — test-takers may be growing more sophisticated.

## Generalist Genes
- **Definition**: The finding from behavioral genetics that the same sets of genes influence multiple cognitive and academic traits (pleiotropy). Genes affecting intelligence also affect academic achievement, learning disabilities, and other cognitive phenotypes.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), p.9, citing Plomin & Kovas (2005)
- **Role in paper**: Supports the argument that intellectual and academic traits share a common genetic basis, reinforcing the centrality of intelligence.

## Warsaw Natural Experiment
- **Definition**: After World War II, Warsaw, Poland's communist government assigned residents to reconstructed districts randomly to prevent social segregation. Firkowska et al. (1978) studied the cognitive outcomes, finding that the correlation between child IQ and social class index was r² = 0.97, and between-school variance was reduced from ~10% to 2.1%.
- **Source**: Detterman (2016), pp.3-4, citing Firkowska et al. (1978)
- **Role in paper**: Demonstrates that even school-level variance can be largely attributed to the non-random distribution of students across schools, and that when this is eliminated, student variance becomes even more dominant (98%).
