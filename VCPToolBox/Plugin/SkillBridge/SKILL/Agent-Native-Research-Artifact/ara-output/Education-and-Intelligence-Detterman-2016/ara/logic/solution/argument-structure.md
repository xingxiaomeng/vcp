# Argument Structure

## Overall Architecture

The paper builds its case through a layered argument moving from intuitive demonstration (thought experiment) through empirical evidence (variance decomposition studies) to implications (future research direction).

## Layer 1: Thought Experiment (Intuitive Demonstration)

**Purpose**: To provide an intuitive, accessible demonstration that student ability differences will always outweigh teacher quality differences under controlled conditions.

- Condition A (Teacher Quality): 50 teachers ranked by quality → 20 students randomly assigned to each → correlate teacher quality rank with mean student gain
  - Predicted correlation: r ≤ 0.32 (≤ 10% variance)
- Condition B (Student Ability): Students ranked by IQ → grouped into 20 by ability → teachers randomly assigned → correlate mean group IQ with mean student gain
  - Predicted correlation: r ≤ 0.95 (≤ 90% variance)

**Key intuition**: Even under ideal measurement conditions, the upper bound of teacher-driven variance is dramatically lower than student-driven variance.

## Layer 2: Empirical Variance Decomposition

**Purpose**: To demonstrate that real-world data confirm the thought experiment's predictions.

| Study | School/Teacher Variance | Student Variance | Notes |
|-------|----------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Coleman Report (1966) | 10%–20% (schools); ~1% (teachers) | 80%–90% | US national sample, >645K students |
| Firkowska et al. (Warsaw, 1978) | 2.1% (schools, after eliminating segregation) | 97.9% | Natural experiment with near-random assignment |
| Angoff & Johnson (1990) | ≤ 7% (institutions) | ≥ 93% | College level (SAT → GRE) |
| Byrne et al. (2010) | ≤ 8% (different teachers) | ≥ 92% | Twin study |
| Chingos & Whitehurst (2014) | 9.2% (all school factors); 3% (teachers) | 90.8% | FL & NC, ~23M data points |

**Key pattern**: Convergent evidence across methodologies, populations, and education levels consistently shows schools/teachers account for ≤ 10% of variance.

## Layer 3: Intelligence as the Dominant Student Factor

**Purpose**: To identify the most important component within the 90% student-attributable variance.

| Evidence | Finding | Implication |
|----------|---------|-------------|
| Deary et al. (2007): GCSE & CAT, >70K students | r = 0.81 between g factors | Intelligence predicts ~66% of achievement g variance |
| Kaufman et al. (2012): Two test batteries | Mean r = 0.83 (range 0.77–0.94) | Cross-battery replication |
| Krapohl et al. (2014): >13K twins | Intelligence alone: 34% of GCSE variance; 8 other traits + intelligence: 45% | Intelligence ≥ other 8 traits combined |
| Lynn & Mikk (2007): National-level TIMSS | r = 0.85–0.93 (uncorrected) | Pattern holds at national level |

**Key finding**: Intelligence accounts for approximately 34%–66% of total achievement variance, making it the single most important known predictor — 6 to 9 times more variance than teachers.

## Layer 4: Causal Direction

**Purpose**: To argue that intelligence drives achievement, not vice versa.

- Cross-lagged studies (Crano et al., 1972; Watkins et al., 2007): Intelligence (T1) → Achievement (T2) significant; Achievement (T1) → Intelligence (T2) not significant
- Mosing et al. (2014): Music ability analogous — practice does not cause ability after controlling for genetics
- Flynn Effect caveat: Environmental improvements can raise raw scores, but this may be cultural sophistication rather than genuine fluid intelligence gains

## Layer 5: The Infrastructure of Intelligence

**Purpose**: To outline what needs to be understood for genuine educational progress.

Three converging research fronts:
1. **Behavioral genetics**: Genes → intelligence pathway; pleiotropy (generalist genes); Gene × Environment interactions
2. **Neuroscience**: P-FIT model; brain development trajectories
3. **Cognition**: Stability of intelligence across lifespan (Deary et al., 2000); working memory as core reasoning component; executive function heritability (Engelhardt et al., 2015)

**Central thesis**: Understanding the "infrastructure of intelligence" (genes → brain → behavior) is the prerequisite for meaningful educational improvement.

## Layer 6: Why This Has Been Ignored

**Purpose**: To address the meta-question of why well-established findings have not changed educational practice.

1. **Illusion of changeability** — Schools seem easier to change than students
2. **Political ideology** — Egalitarian tradition resists accepting individual differences
3. **Professional incentives** — Teacher training and evaluation systems are built around teacher-centered models
4. **Historical inertia** — Education has not fundamentally changed in thousands of years
