# Claims

## C01: SSR resolves the narrow-distribution artifact of direct LLM elicitation
- **Statement**: The Semantic Similarity Rating method (mapping free-text LLM responses to Likert distributions via embedding cosine similarity) produces response distributions that are substantially more realistic than direct Likert rating (DLR), achieving KS similarity > 0.85 with human data compared to DLR's 0.26–0.39.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A replication study on a comparable set of 50+ consumer surveys finds that any alternative elicitation method (including but not limited to DLR, FLR) achieves distributional KS similarity equal to or exceeding SSR.
- **Proof**: [E01, E02, E03]
- **Dependencies**: [A01, A02]
- **Tags**: SSR, response distributions, DLR, Likert elicitation
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C02: SSR achieves high correlation attainment (≈ 90% of human test-retest ceiling)
- **Statement**: SSR-based synthetic consumers recover the relative ranking of product concepts by mean purchase intent at ρ ≈ 90% of the maximum attainable correlation, as benchmarked by human test-retest reliability.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: On an independent set of 30+ consumer surveys, SSR yields a correlation attainment significantly below 70% of the human test-retest ceiling.
- **Proof**: [E01, E02, E03]
- **Dependencies**: [A03]
- **Tags**: correlation attainment, concept ranking, test-retest reliability
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C03: Demographic conditioning (especially age and income) is necessary for valid product ranking
- **Statement**: LLMs prompted without demographic markers achieve high distributional similarity (KS ≈ 0.91) but lose product-ranking signal (ρ ≈ 50%), while conditioning on demographics restores ranking to ρ ≈ 90% – at the cost of slight reduction in distributional similarity.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: An experiment on the same 57 surveys where synthetic consumers without demographic conditioning achieve product-ranking correlation attainment above 70% of the human test-retest ceiling.
- **Proof**: [E04, E05]
- **Dependencies**: []
- **Tags**: demographic conditioning, persona, product ranking
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C04: Zero-shot LLM elicitation outperforms a supervised ML baseline (LightGBM) despite having no access to training survey data
- **Statement**: SSR achieves higher correlation attainment (ρ = 88% vs. 65%) and higher distributional similarity (KS = 0.88 vs. 0.80) than LightGBM classifiers trained on demographic and product features from the same surveys.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A re-analysis using a more powerful supervised model (e.g., XGBoost with hyperparameter optimization, or a neural network) achieves correlation attainment and distributional similarity both exceeding SSR.
- **Proof**: [E06]
- **Dependencies**: [A04]
- **Tags**: zero-shot, ML baseline, LightGBM, comparison
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C05: SSR preserves qualitative richness as a byproduct
- **Statement**: The free-text responses generated by SSR-based synthetic consumers provide detailed, actionable qualitative feedback about product concepts — richer in information than typical human open-ended responses.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A blinded evaluation by 3+ marketing experts finds that human free-text responses are consistently rated as more informative or useful than SSR-generated synthetic responses.
- **Proof**: [E07]
- **Dependencies**: []
- **Tags**: qualitative feedback, synthetic explanations
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C06: SSR generalizes to other Likert-type survey questions (e.g., relevance)
- **Statement**: When applied to the question "How relevant was the concept?" with question-specific reference statement sets, SSR achieves correlation attainment ρ = 82% and distributional similarity KS = 0.81.
- **Status**: hypothesis
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: On 10+ surveys with a non-purchase-intent Likert question, SSR achieves correlation attainment below 50% of the human test-retest ceiling, or distributional similarity below 0.5.
- **Proof**: [E09]
- **Dependencies**: [A01, A02]
- **Tags**: generalization, relevance, survey methodology
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C07: The SSR temperature parameter T optimizes the trade-off between correlation and distributional similarity
- **Statement**: Varying the post-elicitation temperature parameter T in the SSR similarity-to-probability mapping produces a Pareto frontier: higher T increases distributional similarity but decreases correlation attainment, with T ≈ 1 being a reasonable default.
- **Status**: hypothesis
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: A systematic scan of T values on a held-out set of surveys shows no clear trade-off (i.e., both metrics increase or decrease together across T) or finds that the optimal T across metrics is far from 1.0 (e.g., T < 0.3 or T > 3.0).
- **Proof**: [E10]
- **Dependencies**: [A01]
- **Tags**: temperature, SSR parameters, optimization
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27

## C08: SSR replicates human demographic gradients in age and income, but not in gender or region
- **Statement**: SSR-based synthetic consumers reproduce human-like mean purchase intent patterns across age (concave), income (budget-sensitive), product category, source, and price tier — but fail to replicate gender or dwelling region gradients.
- **Status**: supported
- **Provenance**: user
- **Falsification criteria**: In a stratified analysis of 57 surveys, SSR synthetic consumers show mean purchase intent deviations exceeding 0.3 on the Likert scale for age or income subgroups compared to human benchmarks.
- **Proof**: [E11]
- **Dependencies**: []
- **Tags**: demographics, subgroup analysis, age, income
- **Last revised**: 2025-10-27
